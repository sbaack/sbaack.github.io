Civic Hacking and Journalism How data is changing civic engagement

About me

My name is Stefan Baack and in this blog I write about my research on civic hacking and data journalism. I live and work in the Netherlands as a PhD candidate at the University of Groningen (Reseach Centre for Media and Journalism Studies).

At the moment I work on a case study about the British NGO mySociety, one of the oldest and most influential civic hacking organizations. Civic hacking is a relatively new phenomenon that did not receive much attention in Media and Communication research or Journalism studies. As the popularity and the influence of civic hacking is growing, it is important to study its underlying practices and values to develop a better understanding of what civic hacking is about (a question civic hackers are struggling to answer as well), and what larger cultural influence on journalism, public institutions, and civil society it might have.

I’m particularly interested in the cultural influence on journalism by paying attention to where civic hackers and journalists ‘meet’. The term ‘meeting’ has a double meaning here: in a direct sense it means observing where they physically meet or directly communicate with each other; in a more abstract, metaphorical sense it is about paying attention to how they overlap in their practices, in the way they think and so forth. Numerous programs (like Hacks/Hackers or OpenNews) try to bring hackers and journalists together these days, but what is their outcome? How do civic hackers and journalists exchange, pick up, modify, ignore or reject each others practices, concepts or ideas? Using my research on civic hacking at mySociety as a background and trying to get involved in the hacker-journalism scene, I aim to find answers to these questions.
